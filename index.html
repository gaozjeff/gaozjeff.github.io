  • Revit Model of Literatures, Cultures and Languages Building

    In our CAD Graphics and Design course our final project consisted of taking building plans for a given building and modeling the building in Revit. For the first part of our project, we established the grids and levels of our project following the historical building plans to that we would have a baseline to go off for the rest of the model. Following that we imported all of the necessary size beams and columns for each level and began modeling. We started out by laying out the foundational slabs and columns before working our way up level by level and finishing the structural beams. After our structures were finished we began constructing the walls for the exterior and interior and adding interior details such as windows and lights. We did not add mechanical, electrical, or plumbing information as this was outside the scope of our project. As we finished up the interior we then began preparing furniture and other decorations for our renderings.

    Once our Revit model was finished up we started by writing our report detailing our modeling process along with screenshots of our work. We also completed a basic SketchUp model of the building and the exterior including two more surrounding buildings and the main quad. Finally, at the end of our report we included all the printed and annotated sheets for reference and comparison with the actual sheets

  • Traffic Engineering Network Flow Optimization

    The Traffic Engineering Network Flow project was assigned in our Systems Engineering and Economic class. We were given the scenario where everybody tries to immediately leave a home basketball game at the University of Illinois, we were tasked with modeling several different scenarios about traffic flow. For the first scenario we assumed that all roads have infinite capacity and drivers want to minimize their travel distance. Our subtasks included weighting east and west exits to model higher flow towards the cities of Champaign and Urbana and closing a parking lot to see what difference it would make on the flow of traffic. For the second task we modeled what would happen if we increased the parking lot demand by 20% out of one of a given parking lot. Our third task had us model the network now accounting for road capacity. In order to do this we utilized Google Maps satellite imagery to count the number of lanes for each road in the network and assumed a static value of 450 cars per hour per lane. We modelled this by creating a list of lanes for each link and then assigning them the static value of cars per hour per lane. Our fourth and final task had us model how the network would change from the addition of one lane. We wanted to determine the economic feasibility of the addition of one or even two lanes on a given link.

  • Statistical Analysis of Water Discharge and Gage Height

    Our Engineering Risk and Uncertainty class project was to analyze whether there was a statistical correlation between our given data sets. Our group worked with a set of USGS data on Boneyard Creek, a tributary of the Vermillion River in Illinois, which has had historic flooding problems. Using USGS data we wanted to test the null hypothesis that there was a linear regression between the measured amount of water discharge in cubic feet and the measured gage height from the measuring station along the creek. Using R, we imported the CSV file containing data from 1992 and began to analyze it. First we created summary tables of different statistics such as mean and standard deviation for each variable; while also making sure to take into account two-variable statistics such as covariance. We then plotted some of our data including a scatterplot of our data. After creating a basic scatterplot of our data we noticed outliers and filtered our CSV to only include gage heights that fell within a specific range correlating to a specific time period of measurement. Our R^2 value came out to 0.645 indicating a linear relationship.
